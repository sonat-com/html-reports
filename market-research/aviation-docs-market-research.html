01

The bottom line, up front

Verdict

Aviation documentation splits into two worlds. Sonat can genuinely win one of them and should not chase the other.

Don't fight the regulated core. The approved exposition manuals — CAME MOE OM‑A/B/C/D — are hard-mandated by EASA to be regulator-reviewed, PDF-delivered, revision-controlled and audit-tracked. Specialists like Web Manuals and Comply365 own this with live compliance libraries, controlled crew distribution and Type B EFB apps. Sonat has none of that machinery, and building it is a multi-year lift.

Win the surrounding knowledge tier instead. Every operator also runs a large body of non-approved content — ground-school & training reference, internal SOP knowledge bases, partner/passenger-facing docs — that today lives in scattered PDFs, Google Drive and email. That is squarely Sonat's job: Google Docs authoring, 184-language translation, custom-domain publishing, approval workflows and RAG answers. Land there with SAF; expand across its brands.

02

Two clusters, one honest line between them

Every doc an operator produces falls on one side of this line. Sonat only belongs on the right.

Cluster A · Sonat does not fit

Regulated core & engineering data

  • Exposition & ops manuals: CAME, MOE, OM‑A/B/C/D, training/ops manuals — regulator-approved, controlled.
  • Engineering / maintenance data: OEM AMM, CMM, IPC, Service Bulletins, wiring — structured to S1000D / ATA iSpec 2200.
Owned by Web Manuals & Comply365 (manuals) and TRAX / AMOS / Veryon / IFS Maintenix (engineering data). Regulatory moat, not just a feature gap.
Cluster B · Sonat's opening

Operational & knowledge content

  • Non-approved knowledge: ground-school reference, SOP knowledge bases, internal wikis, onboarding.
  • Outward-facing docs: partner, client and passenger documentation — multilingual, public, searchable.
Underserved by the specialists (they're built for controlled internal distribution, not public multilingual publishing). This is Sonat's core competency.

Who serves this today

Two specialist tiers. Sonat competes with neither head-on — but the manual tier is the reference point buyers will hold Sonat against.

Controlled operational & compliance manuals — Sonat's nearest neighbours

Web Manuals Sweden

The market reference for authoring + compliance-linking + controlled distribution. Live compliance libraries (EASA/FAA/CASA/IOSA/ICAO) with rule-to-paragraph linking & change alerts; Type B EFB app; offline; read-confirmation; full audit trail. Serves airlines, business aviation, helicopter/UAV operators, MROs and ATOs.

Scale: 750+ companies · 50,000+ users · 500,000 manuals
Price: Essentials ~€4,000/yr (Reader + Editor licenses); Compliance Library +€50–60/mo

Comply365 + Vistair

Merged Jan 2024 (Liberty Hall + Insight Partners). Three connected systems — ContentManager365 (docs/distribution), SafetyManager365, TrainingManager365 — linking procedure changes to training & safety. Multi-OEM ingest (Boeing/Airbus/Rolls-Royce), offline latest-content viewer, regulator access.

Scale: 50+ airlines + defense / rail / MRO
Customers: Aer Lingus, easyJet, Avianca, SunExpress; Vistair ran BA's 3,600-iPad rollout

Yonder Switzerland

"Yonder Mind" — XML-powered. Auto-syncs OEM manuals (FCOM/MEL/QRH/FCTM) against operator customisations, flagging conflicts. Role-specific "smart viewer" shows only relevant content; offline app for pilots, cabin crew & engineers.

Angle: operators juggling multiple OEMs
Model: documentation-as-a-service

Engineering / maintenance tech pubs — entirely out of Sonat's scope

Cluster A · reference only TRAX eMRO · AMOS (Swiss-AS) · IFS Maintenix · Veryon are M&E/MRO platforms built for tier-1 carriers and MROs. They handle the structured engineering-data pipeline (S1000D / ATA iSpec 2200), work orders, airworthiness tracking and parts. This is an ERP-grade data problem, not a document-authoring one — Sonat has no role here and should not position into it.

The SAF trial play

Land narrow and honest in the knowledge tier; expand across the group's brands and languages. Never over-claim the regulated core — it poisons trust.

1

Land

Target one painful, non-regulated body of knowledge — the internal SOP/knowledge base or X-Aero's ground-school reference — that lives today in Drive, PDFs and email. Fast time-to-value, zero regulatory risk.

2

Demo the wedge, not the world

Show Google-Docs → publish, one-click 184-language translation (FR/EN/AR), a custom-domain portal, an approval workflow, and a RAG answer over their own content. These are the exact five things the specialists do poorly or not at all.

3

Expand across brands & audiences

SAF Hélicoptères, SAF International, Aero Support Force, X-Aero — each has public-facing and internal knowledge. Multilingual multi-brand publishing is a natural land-and-expand; viewers are free, so growth isn't gated by seat cost.

4

Draw the line explicitly

Tell them plainly Sonat is not replacing their CAME/MOE/OM controlled-manual process. Naming the boundary builds the credibility that wins the knowledge-tier deal — and avoids a failed regulated-doc pilot that sinks the account.

Objections you'll hear — and the honest answers

"Is this an aviation-grade controlled-manual system for our approved documents?"
No — and we won't pretend otherwise. Your CAME, MOE and OM suite stay in your specialist tool or DGAC-approved process. Sonat owns the large body of operational and training knowledge sitting around those approved manuals, which no one has purpose-built for you today.
"We already have Web Manuals / a compliance tool — why add Sonat?"
Those tools are built for controlled internal distribution, not public multilingual publishing, non-technical authoring or RAG self-service. Different job. Sonat sits alongside them, not against them — and its viewers are free, so it scales to your whole workforce and audience without per-seat cost.
If Sonat ever wanted the regulated tier It would need read-confirmation/acknowledgement tracking, an aviation-grade audit trail & e-signature, controlled distribution, and an offline EFB-class app — a multi-year investment against entrenched, audited incumbents. Not today's positioning; flagged here so the boundary is a deliberate choice, not an accident.

Glossary

CAME
Continuing Airworthiness Management Exposition — the CAMO's approved procedures manual (AMC1 CAMO.A.300).
MOE
Maintenance Organisation Exposition — the Part-145 maintenance org's approved manual (§145.A.70).
OM-A/B/C/D
The four-part airline Operations Manual (general, aircraft, route/area, training).
CAMO
Continuing Airworthiness Management Organisation — manages an aircraft's airworthiness.
ATO
Approved Training Organisation — EASA-approved flight/ground training provider (Part-ORA).
SMS
Safety Management System — hazard ID, risk management, safety assurance (ICAO Annex 19).
EFB
Electronic Flight Bag — tablet/app delivering manuals & charts to crew; Type B is a regulatory class.
MEL
Minimum Equipment List — what may be inoperative and still dispatch legally.
IOSA
IATA Operational Safety Audit — the industry audit program; mandates a Document Control System.
S1000D
International XML spec for structured technical publications / maintenance data.
ATA iSpec 2200
Aviation standard for structuring & exchanging engineering/maintenance data (AMM, CMM, IPC…).
DGAC
France's civil aviation authority — SAF's competent authority under EASA rules.
