Executive Summary

Spend eased to $77.70 (−10.0% week-over-week), but delivery pulled back further: Impressions fell −18.4% and Reach −17.2%. Because reach contracted faster than spend, unit costs rose — CPM +10.4% and CPC (all) +15.6%. Link Clicks dropped −13.5% to 364.

The encouraging signal is engagement quality on the traffic the campaign did buy: CTR (Link Click) improved +6.0% and Cost per Link Click rose only +4.1% — far less than the +15.6% jump in CPC (all), meaning the audience that was reached clicked through to the site at a healthy, slightly better rate. Frequency held essentially flat (1.17 vs 1.19), so this is a delivery-volume story, not an audience-fatigue one. A single registration/lead conversion was recorded this week (vs none last week) — too small a sample to draw conclusions from.

Recommendations testable hypotheses, not directives

Hypothesis 1 · Delivery

Hold budget steady next week and watch whether unit costs normalize

Spend fell −10.0% while Impressions (−18.4%) and Reach (−17.2%) fell more, which is the arithmetic behind CPM +10.4% and CPC (all) +15.6%. From these aggregate totals alone we can see what moved but not why (no daily series or auction-level data here).

Test: restore budget to the prior ~$86/wk level and observe whether CPM/CPC revert. Expected impact: recovers lost reach and clarifies whether the cost rise was a pacing artifact or a genuine auction shift — informs the next budget decision overall.

Hypothesis 2 · Creative / Audience

The on-site click intent is holding — lean into it, don't pull back

CTR (Link Click) improved +6.0% and Cost per Link Click rose only +4.1%, versus +15.6% for CPC (all). The traffic the campaign bought stayed on-objective and barely more expensive per link click than last week. Per our guardrail, the higher average CPM/CPC is not a reason to cut this campaign.

Test: keep the current creative/audience live and consider a modest reach expansion. Expected impact: protects the improving link-click rate while re-buying the reach lost this week, lifting overall link-click volume.

Hypothesis 3 · Measurement

If registrations matter, measure them with a conversion-optimized setup

One Complete Registration / lead was recorded this week (vs none last week). At N=1 there is no reliable cost-per-lead to report. The campaign is optimized for Link Clicks, not registrations, so Meta isn't bidding toward that outcome.

Test: stand up a small conversion-optimized ad set (registration event) alongside the traffic campaign. Expected impact: produces a trustworthy cost-per-registration signal so future budget shifts are decided on conversions, not click proxies.

Hypothesis 4 · Monitoring

Treat the cost rise as a watch item, not a trigger to pause

Frequency is flat (1.17 vs 1.19), so this is not audience saturation. With a single week of aggregate movement and no breakdowns, the prudent action is to monitor CPM/CPC over the next 1–2 weeks before reallocating.

Test: re-check CPM/CPC and CTR (Link Click) next week against this baseline. Expected impact: avoids over-reacting to one week of noise on a low-spend account (~$78/wk).

Methodology

This is a weekly executive digest built from aggregate account- and campaign-level totals for Jun 20–26, 2026 and the preceding equal-length window (Jun 13–19, 2026), pulled from the Meta Graph API (v21.0). It reports what changed week-over-week. It deliberately does not attempt deep root-cause diagnosis (marginal-CPA / Breakdown-Effect, learning-phase, auction-overlap or relevance-ranking analysis), because the supporting data — daily time series, placement/audience breakdowns, and ad-set/ad-level detail — is not present in this pull. Metric display names follow the meta-ads-analyzer conventions; audience is reported as Accounts Center accounts; "Clicks (all)" denotes total interactions while "Link Clicks" denotes offsite link clicks. All money is in USD. No purchase/ROAS metrics are shown because the objective is traffic (link clicks) and purchase conversions are not tracked for this window.
