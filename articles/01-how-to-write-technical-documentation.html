Technical Writing

How to Write Technical Documentation People Actually Read

You've been asked to document a product, a process, or a system — and the blank page is staring back. You know the material cold, but turning what's in your head into something a reader can follow is a different skill entirely. Good documentation isn't about writing more; it's about writing so the right answer is easy to find and easy to act on.

This guide walks through how to write technical documentation from first outline to published page: how to structure it, how to keep it readable, and how to maintain it so it stays useful. No jargon, no theory for its own sake — just the practices that separate docs people rely on from docs they abandon.

What technical documentation is (and what makes it "good")

Technical documentation is any content that explains how something works or how to use it — user manuals, help centers, API references, runbooks, policies, and onboarding guides. Good technical documentation lets a reader complete a task or answer a question without asking a human for help. That's the whole test. If your docs deflect the support ticket, they worked.

Everything below serves that single goal. A practice only counts as a best practice if it makes the answer faster to find or easier to trust.

Start with the reader, not the feature

The most common documentation mistake is writing from the author's point of view — organized by how the product is built rather than by what the reader is trying to do. Flip it.

Before you write a word, answer three questions:

  • Who is reading this? A first-time user and an experienced admin need different pages, not the same page with more caveats.
  • What are they trying to accomplish? Document tasks ("reset a password"), not features ("the credentials module").
  • What do they already know? Assume less than you think. Federal plain-language guidance is blunt about this: content has to be written for its specific audience, and the only way to know it works is to test whether that audience actually understands it.

Write the page title as the reader's goal. "How to invite a teammate" beats "Team management settings" every time, because it matches the words a reader would actually search for.

Structure documentation in small, self-contained topics

Structure is where most documentation quietly fails. Long, scrolling pages that cover ten things force readers to hunt. The fix is modular, topic-based structure: one topic answers one question and stands on its own.

A reliable technical documentation structure looks like this:

  • One topic, one job. Each page covers a single task or concept. If a heading needs the word "and," it's probably two topics.
  • Lead with the answer. Put the conclusion or the first step at the top and the background below — the "inverted pyramid" that news writers use. Readers who already know the context can act immediately; those who need more can keep reading.
  • Group topics into a shallow menu. A clear two-level menu (section → topic) beats a five-level tree nobody can navigate. Aim for breadth over depth.
  • Cross-link related topics inline, where the reader hits the need — not in a pile of links at the bottom.

Modular topics also age better. When one step changes, you edit one small topic instead of surgically editing a 3,000-word mega-page — and, increasingly, well-structured topics are what AI search tools can retrieve and answer from cleanly.

Write for scanning, because nobody reads top to bottom

Here's the uncomfortable truth backed by decades of usability research: people don't read documentation, they scan it. In Nielsen Norman Group's classic eye-tracking work, 79% of users scanned any new page they landed on, and only 16% read word by word. Rewriting the same content to be concise improved measured usability by 58%; making it scannable improved it by 47%; doing both together improved it by 124%.

So write for the scanner:

  • Front-load keywords in headings and the first few words of each paragraph.
  • Keep paragraphs to one idea and roughly three to five lines.
  • Use bulleted lists when items are parallel and there are more than a couple.
  • Bold the words that carry meaning — sparingly, so the emphasis still means something.
  • Cut ruthlessly. If a sentence doesn't help the reader act, delete it.

A quick objective check helps here: run a draft through a readability score like Flesch Reading Ease and aim for plain, everyday phrasing. The score isn't a grade to chase for its own sake — it's a smoke alarm for sentences that got too long or too dense.

Keep a lightweight style guide

Consistency is what makes a set of pages feel like one trustworthy source instead of a dozen voices. You don't need a 60-page manual — a one-page style guide covering a few decisions goes a long way. Most well-run documentation teams settle on a handful of habits worth borrowing:

  • Second person and active voice. "Select Save," not "the Save button should be selected." It's shorter and tells the reader exactly who does what.
  • Present tense. "The page opens," not "the page will open."
  • Consistent terms. Pick one word for each thing — "sign in" or "log in," never both — and stick to it.
  • Sentence-case headings and consistent formatting for buttons, menus, and code.

Write these choices down once. New contributors follow the guide instead of guessing, and your documentation reads as one voice even when five people write it.

Show, don't just tell

Some things are faster to see than to read. Screenshots, short diagrams, and annotated images cut the number of words a reader has to parse — as long as they clarify rather than decorate. A good visual sits right next to the step it illustrates, carries descriptive alt text for accessibility and search, and gets updated when the interface does. A stale screenshot is worse than none, because it quietly tells the reader your docs can't be trusted.

Make it maintainable, not just correct today

Documentation isn't a one-time deliverable; it's a living asset that decays the moment the product moves on. The best-written page is worthless six months later if nobody can tell it's out of date. Build maintenance in from the start:

  • Keep a single source of truth. One canonical topic per subject, so there's no second copy drifting out of sync.
  • Track versions. Being able to see what changed — and restore a previous version — turns "who edited this?" from a mystery into a click.
  • Review on a schedule and after every release. Tie doc updates to your release process so they ship together, not months apart.
  • Plan for translation early. If you serve a global audience, clean modular topics are far cheaper to translate and keep in sync than sprawling pages.

A simple workflow to write technical documentation

Pulling it together, here's a repeatable loop you can run for any new page:

  • 1. Define the reader and the task in one sentence each.
  • 2. Draft the outline as headings — one topic per heading, phrased as the reader's goal.
  • 3. Write the steps or explanation, answer-first, in plain language.
  • 4. Add visuals where a picture beats a paragraph.
  • 5. Edit for scanning — cut, tighten, check the readability score.
  • 6. Run it past a real reader who matches your audience, and fix what trips them up.
  • 7. Publish, then schedule the next review.

Follow that loop and each page gets easier to write, because the structure does the heavy lifting.

The shift that matters

Writing technical documentation well isn't about mastering prose — it's about respecting how people actually use docs. They arrive with a task, they scan for the answer, and they leave the moment they're unblocked. Structure your content into small self-contained topics, write it to be scanned, keep the style consistent, and maintain it like the living asset it is, and you'll write documentation people trust enough to rely on instead of routing around.

Start with your next page. Pick one task your readers keep asking about, write the single topic that answers it, and publish — then let the workflow above carry the rest.
