How to Create a Knowledge Base Your Team Will Actually Use

Published June 8, 2026  ·  7 min read

Your team answers the same five questions every week. A new employee spends their first three days hunting for the onboarding process. A customer emails support about something that is already documented — somewhere.

If any of that sounds familiar, you are ready to create a knowledge base. This guide shows you how to create a knowledge base from scratch: how to plan it, structure it, write good articles, choose the right tool, and keep it useful long after launch.

What Is a Knowledge Base?

A knowledge base is a centralized, searchable collection of information your team or customers need to do their jobs or use your product. It is different from a shared drive or wiki in one key way: it is designed to be found, not just stored.

A good knowledge base surfaces the right answer to a specific question, whether a customer is troubleshooting a product, an employee is looking up a policy, or a new hire is learning a process.

Step 1: Define Your Purpose and Audience

Before writing a single article, answer two questions:

  • Who is it for? Internal teams (employees, new hires, operations), external users (customers, partners), or both?
  • What problem does it solve? Reducing support tickets, onboarding faster, standardizing processes, improving product adoption?

The answers shape every decision that follows — the tone of your articles, the categories you create, and the tool you choose.

Internal vs. External Knowledge Bases

An internal knowledge base is private and houses information for your team: HR policies, IT procedures, sales playbooks, onboarding guides. Access is usually restricted to employees or specific departments.

An external knowledge base is public-facing and helps customers help themselves: product guides, FAQs, troubleshooting steps, setup tutorials. It reduces the volume of repetitive support queries and is often the first place a customer looks before contacting support.

Many organizations run both. The best documentation platforms let you manage them from a single workspace, publish each to different audiences, and keep the two in sync where content overlaps.

Step 2: Plan Your Knowledge Base Structure

Structure is the part most teams skip — and the one that determines whether users can actually find what they need.

Start with user goals, not team org charts. The categories in your knowledge base should reflect the questions your audience asks, not your internal department names. "Getting Started," "Billing," and "Troubleshooting" serve users better than "Product Team Output" or "Support Team FAQ."

A simple starting structure for most knowledge bases:

  • Getting Started — onboarding articles, first steps, account setup
  • Core Features / Processes — the main tasks users or employees need to do
  • Troubleshooting / FAQs — answers to the most common questions
  • Reference — technical specs, glossaries, policies, detailed configurations
  • Updates / What's New — changelog entries, policy revisions

Keep the hierarchy shallow. Two levels of nesting (category → articles) handles most use cases. Three levels starts to bury content; four means users will rely on search rather than navigation, which is a sign the structure has failed.

Knowledge Base Structure Best Practices

  • Limit each category to 5–15 articles to start. Expand as the knowledge base grows.
  • Use consistent naming: all titles should follow the same format — either action-based ("How to reset your password") or noun-based ("Password reset") — not a mix.
  • Mirror the structure in your navigation menu exactly. Hidden categories are effectively missing.

Step 3: Write Articles That Actually Answer the Question

Most knowledge base articles fail at the same place: they describe a feature rather than answering a question. Write from the reader's situation, not from the product's perspective.

What a Good Knowledge Base Article Looks Like

One article, one question. Resist the urge to answer five related questions in one document. "Setting up your account" is a good title. "Setting up your account, managing billing, and inviting users" is three articles.

Answer first, explain second. Open with the direct answer or the key step. Background and context come after. Users scan for the fix before they read for understanding.

Use numbered steps for processes. If a task requires doing things in order, use a numbered list. Unordered bullets suggest any sequence is fine. Pick the right format for the task.

Include what to expect. After completing a step, what does the user see? "Click Save. A confirmation banner appears at the top of the screen." Saves support tickets.

End with next steps. Where do readers go from here? Link to the next logical article. This is how you turn a one-question visit into a user who understands your product.

Knowledge Base Article Best Practices

  • Keep articles under 800 words when possible. Longer is fine for complex topics; always ask whether it should be two articles instead.
  • Add a short introduction (2–3 lines) that confirms the reader is in the right place.
  • Every screenshot needs alt text. It is good accessibility and keeps the article usable if images fail to load.
  • Review articles whenever the underlying product or process changes. Stale documentation erodes trust faster than no documentation.

Step 4: Choose the Right Documentation Tool

The tool you choose determines what your team can do without a developer. Look for these capabilities:

Collaborative editing. Your subject matter experts should be able to write and edit without learning a new tool. Platforms that integrate with Google Docs remove the biggest barrier to getting knowledge out of people's heads.

Search that works. Users find information through search more often than navigation. Full-text search with smart ranking is non-negotiable. If users cannot find articles through search, the structure does not matter.

Publishing control. You need to be able to publish to a custom domain, control who can view which content, and update articles without taking anything offline.

Version history. Documentation changes. Version control lets you track what changed, when, and restore a previous version if something goes wrong.

SEO fundamentals. External knowledge bases should be findable through search engines. Meta tags, sitemaps, and clean URLs are not optional features.

Step 5: Publish, Promote, and Maintain

A knowledge base is not a project with a launch date — it is a system that needs ongoing maintenance. These habits keep it useful:

Seed it before launching. Never launch an empty knowledge base. Start with the 10 most frequently asked questions, one getting-started guide, and a troubleshooting article for the most common issue. Thin is fine; empty is not.

Tell people it exists. For internal knowledge bases, announce it in the tools your team already uses. For external bases, link to it from your product UI, your support portal, and your email signatures. Discovery does not happen automatically.

Track what people search for — and what they don't find. Failed searches are the best editorial signal you have. If users search for "invoice" and find nothing, that is the next article to write.

Set a review cadence. Every article should have an owner and a review date. Quarterly is right for most content; monthly for fast-changing areas like product release notes.

Building an Internal Knowledge Base for Employees

Internal knowledge bases have a different challenge than customer-facing ones: the people who know the most are often the least available to write it down.

A few approaches that work:

  • Start with onboarding. New hires experience knowledge gaps in sequence. Follow one new hire through their first two weeks and document every question they ask. You will have 20+ articles by the end.
  • Capture knowledge during team meetings. Decisions made in meetings rarely make it to documentation. A brief post-meeting summary, added directly to the knowledge base, costs five minutes and saves future confusion.
  • Make contribution easy. If contributing requires learning a new tool, different teams find workarounds in whatever they already use — and nothing will be findable later. Pick a platform your whole team can use from day one.
  • Give ownership, not just access. Assign each section of the knowledge base to a specific team or person. Collective ownership means no ownership.
