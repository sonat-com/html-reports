How to Write Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs): A Step-by-Step Guide

Every team has a "way we do things." The problem is that the way usually lives in one person's head, three chat threads, and a document nobody has opened since last year. When that person is out — or the process changes and no one updates the doc — the work slows down and mistakes creep in.

Standard operating procedures fix that. Written well, an SOP turns tribal knowledge into a repeatable process anyone can follow the same way, every time. This guide walks through what an SOP is, how to choose a format, and a practical, step-by-step method for writing procedures your team will actually use — and keep current.

What is a standard operating procedure?

A standard operating procedure (SOP) is a set of step-by-step instructions that documents how to carry out a routine task the same way every time. Its job is to make performance consistent, reduce errors and miscommunication, and help people meet quality and regulatory requirements without having to ask.

It helps to separate two words people often blur together:

  • A policy is a high-level rule about what is or isn't allowed — the "what" and "why."
  • A procedure is the ground-level "how" — the exact steps to carry the policy out.

"Return requests must be approved by a manager" is a policy. The numbered steps a support agent follows to log, route, and close that return request is the procedure. You need both, but this guide is about writing the second one well.

Why well-written SOPs are worth the effort

Documented procedures do more than tidy up your operations. Done properly, they:

  • Produce consistent quality. When everyone follows the same steps, output stops depending on who happened to do the work that day.
  • Speed up onboarding. New hires can answer their own questions from the procedure instead of interrupting a colleague, so they get productive faster.
  • Support compliance and audits. Clear, controlled procedures help you meet standards such as ISO 9001 and give auditors documented evidence of how work is actually done.
  • Preserve knowledge. When someone leaves, their expertise stays with the team instead of walking out the door.

The catch is that these benefits only show up if the SOP is clear, findable, and current. An outdated procedure is worse than none at all — people follow the wrong steps with full confidence. That's why the writing process below ends with review and control, not with hitting "save."

Choose the right SOP format

There's no single correct layout. Match the format to how complex the task is:

  • Simple step list. A numbered or bulleted sequence. Best for short, linear tasks where each step follows the last — processing a refund, closing the register, publishing a release note.
  • Hierarchical steps. Main steps with indented sub-steps underneath. Use this when a task is longer and some steps need detail or conditions without drowning the reader in one flat list.
  • Flowchart. A visual map of the process. Reach for this when the path branches — "if the order is over $500, route to finance; otherwise auto-approve." Decisions are far easier to read as a diagram than as nested bullets.

Pick one format and use it consistently across your library. A predictable structure is part of what makes procedures easy to scan under pressure.

How to write a standard operating procedure, step by step

Here is a repeatable method you can apply to any procedure, from a two-minute checklist to a regulated workflow.

1. Define the purpose and scope

Start by answering two questions in writing: What problem does this procedure solve, and where does it begin and end? Name the task, who performs it, and what's explicitly out of scope. A tight scope keeps the SOP focused and stops one procedure from quietly turning into three.

2. Talk to the people who do the work

The fastest way to write an SOP nobody follows is to write it alone at your desk. Sit with the people who actually perform the task and capture how they really do it — including the shortcuts and edge cases the official version forgets. Share an early draft and fold in their corrections. Procedures built with the team get followed; procedures handed to the team get ignored.

3. Write clear, testable steps

Draft the procedure as a sequence of concrete actions, one action per step. A few rules that keep steps usable:

  • Start each step with a verb — "Open," "Select," "Confirm."
  • Write for the least experienced person who'll use it. Plain language beats jargon, and readability matters more than sounding official.
  • Make each step observable. A reader should be able to tell when a step is done.
  • Add screenshots or a short clip for anything visual — a labeled screenshot removes more confusion than a paragraph of description.

The test: hand the draft to someone who has never done the task and watch them follow it without asking questions. Every question they ask is a step you need to fix.

4. Add the supporting elements

Around the steps, a complete SOP usually includes:

  • A title and identifier so the document can be referenced and tracked.
  • Purpose and scope, from step 1.
  • Roles and responsibilities — who does what, and who approves.
  • A short glossary for any terms or acronyms.
  • Materials, tools, or access needed before starting.
  • Troubleshooting notes for the things that commonly go wrong.

Keep these light. Supporting sections exist to serve the steps, not to pad the page.

5. Review, approve, and control the document

A procedure isn't finished when it's written — it's finished when it's approved and controlled. Before an SOP goes live, route it to the right reviewers to confirm it's accurate, safe, and compliant. After it's approved, control it: keep one authoritative version, track changes, and make sure the old copy can't be mistaken for the current one.

This is where a real approval workflow earns its keep. Instead of emailing a document around and hoping the right people sign off, you want the review, the approval, and the version history attached to the document itself.

How often should you review SOPs?

Set a fixed review cycle rather than waiting for something to break. A common cadence is every 6 to 12 months, plus an immediate review whenever the underlying process, tool, or regulation changes. Give every procedure a named owner responsible for that review — an SOP with no owner is an SOP that quietly goes stale. Recording a review date and the reviewer on each document turns "is this still right?" from a guess into a fact.

Common mistakes that make SOPs fail

  • Writing for the wrong reader. If only the expert can follow it, it isn't a procedure — it's a reminder.
  • Never updating it. Processes change; unmaintained SOPs drift from reality until people stop trusting them.
  • Burying it. A procedure no one can find won't be used. Store SOPs in one searchable place, not scattered across drives and inboxes.
  • No approval trail. Without a record of who approved what and when, you can't prove control during an audit.
  • Padding. Long isn't thorough. Cut anything that doesn't help the reader complete the task.

Start with one procedure

You don't need to document everything this quarter. Pick the single task that breaks most often when the wrong person does it, and write that SOP using the steps above: scope it, build it with the team, write testable steps, add the essentials, and put it through review. Then set its next review date. Do that a handful of times and you'll have something more valuable than a folder of documents — a set of procedures your team trusts and keeps current.
